1.Introduction

This project is conducted in collaboration with Professor Wu Changsheng’s laboratory, which partners with clinicians and students from the Saw Swee Hock School Of Public Health. This project is part of an ongoing study on developing a tool to detect MCI biomarkers in patients. The overarching goal of the collaborative project is to develop an advanced, non-invasive wireless wearable mechano-acoustic sensor system to monitor vocal attributes and gait patterns for the early detection of MCI.

 

Figure 1: Task Distribution Among Research Team

Professor Wu’s laboratory developed a custom mechano-acoustic wearable sensor (“Research Sensor”) designed to capture vibration and motion signals from the throat and lower limb. The sensor integrates a three-axis accelerometer within a silicone encapsulation, making it lightweight, waterproof, and comfortable for continuous use. Due to quantity limitations of the research sensor, the group has decided to use the MetaBase sensor (Figure 2) for preliminary data collection and testing.

Figure 2 : Devices Used

1.1 Context and Motivation

Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) is widely recognised as an intermediate stage between normal age-related cognitive decline and dementia. [Eshkoor et al.,2015]. It is characterised by noticeable deficits in memory, attention, and judgment that are greater than expected for a person’s age, yet not severe enough to significantly interfere with daily activities [Cipriani et al.2020]. Individuals with MCI are able to carry out daily activities, but may experience increasing difficulty in performing complex tasks.

MCI is a critical focus area for research because it represents a potential window for early intervention before irreversible neurodegeneration occurs. While some individuals may recover normal cognitive function, numerous longitudinal studies have shown that people with MCI are at a significantly higher risk of progressing to dementia or Alzheimer’s disease (Petersen et al., 2018; Jongsiriyanyong & Limpawattana, 2018).

In Singapore, approximately 12.5% of adults aged 60 and above are estimated to have MCI (Subramaniam et al., 2015). With the country’s rapidly ageing population, this prevalence and its associated healthcare burden is expected to increase. The Ministry of Health (MOH) projects that by 2030, as many as 152,000 people could be living with dementia, incurring an additional S$106 million in annual healthcare expenditure. The median annual cost of care for MCI patients is approximately S$13,800, including medical consultations, imaging scans, and long-term management. [cite]

The growing prevalence of MCI and dementia highlights the urgent need for early, accessible, and cost-effective detection methods.

Current screening and diagnostic procedures include Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) or Positron Emission Tomography (PET) brain scans, neuropsychological testing, and biochemical analysis of cerebrospinal fluid. While these are definitive and accurate, they are expensive, invasive, and resource-intensive. These hinder widespread adoption of screening for MCI, especially for early or community-based screening. Additionally, such assessments are typically performed only after cognitive symptoms become clinically apparent. Early diagnosis remains a challenge, as subtle physiological and behavioural changes frequently go unnoticed in routine clinical visits. [Wenier et al.,2023]

Given these challenges, there is a need to develop a non-invasive, scalable, and affordable tool that can continuously monitor subtle indicators of cognitive decline.

1.3  Project Objectives

As previously mentioned, this project forms part of a broader interdisciplinary collaboration aimed at the early detection of MCI through digital biomarkers. As such, the overarching research problem statement is: “How might we make the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment more timely, affordable, and non-invasive?”.

The purpose of this research is to contribute toward developing a reliable and accessible screening method for MCI using wearable digital sensors. By enabling early identification, the project seeks to facilitate timely intervention and reduce the long-term healthcare burden associated with dementia-related conditions.

Within this collaborative research, our team’s specific objectives focus on investigating the potential of gait and speech as physiological biomarkers for MCI detection. We aim to explore how measurable differences in movement and vocal vibration patterns can serve as early indicators for cognitive decline. As such, our project aims to address the following problem statements:

“How might we use accelerometer signals collected from the Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) to identify gait parameters associated with MCI?”

“How might we analyse recorded speech vibration signals from accelerometers to extract features that distinguish individuals with MCI from healthy individuals?"

Through this research project, we aim to validate the feasibility of these non-invasive sensor-based indicators to assess their potential integration into a continuous monitoring, wearable sensor system for early MCI screening in Singapore.

2. Gait

2.1 Introduction to Gait Concepts

Gait refers to one’s walking patterns and has distinct features. Gait analysis involves measuring distinct features such as step time, stride time, distance, and stability. Through measuring the features, gait events such as “heel strike” and “toe off” can be identified. Figure 4 reflects the stages in a gait cycle.

Gait features obtained from accelerometers can tell us about different phases of gait such as heel strike and toe off (Mayagoitia et al., 2002) (Prisco et.al (2025)).

Gait is increasingly recognised as a sensitive indicator of cognitive health because it depends on complex brain networks that control attention, executive function, and motor coordination. In individuals with MCI, disruptions in these networks can lead to subtle but measurable changes in walking patterns. Studies consistently report slower gait speed, greater stride time variability, and longer double support duration among MCI groups compared to healthy controls. Collectively, these studies suggest that gait reflects both motor and cognitive integrity. Since such alterations often emerge before noticeable memory decline, gait analysis offers a promising non-invasive MCI screening tool to detect early cognitive impairment.

2.3 Data Collection & Prototype

Since the research sensor collects data via a 3-axis accelerometer, only the accelerometer was activated on the Metabase sensor. The Metabase sensor was placed on the thigh and set to 100 Hz sampling rate to recreate previous gait data collection protocol. Accelerometer data was successfully collected and analysed by converting raw 3 axis data to thigh gait angles.

Figure 5 : Thigh Angle Gait Plot

Left: Test data, Right: Established Literature

(Abhayasinghe & Murray, 2014)

In the established thigh gait cycle, gait phases are distinguished within a very close margin. This is because device placement is further away from the main joints (knee, ankle, hip) commonly used for gait analysis. In the case of MCI patients, this threshold is reduced even more due to irregular gait, causing phases to be similar to each other and hence increase the chance of unclear event detection.

Due to these limitations, we experimented to place the sensor at a different location, the ankle (lower lateral shank) to collect gait data (Panebianco et al., 2020; Vargas-Valencia et al., 2016; Trojaniello et al., 2014) .

Testing encompassed securing the Metabase sensor to the ankle and performing daily activities (walking in a straight line, up/down slope and up/down stairs). From the ankle data collected, significant gait features were identifiable from raw and minimally processed data. Additionally, ankle gait was distinct, cyclic and resilient to daily activities.

Figure 6: Raw Accelerometer Data (x, y, z,magnitude)

Furthermore, literature shows that raw accelerometer gait (1-axis) can be used to differentiate between normal and altered walk; and, a conversion to frequency domain can enable the quantification of cadence associated with normal and altered walk as shown in Figure 7. (Canonico et al., 2023b)

Figure 7 : Walking characteristics from raw and FFT accelerometer data

This substantiates the idea that ankle data is more significant for our purpose of MCI gait detection from daily movement.

Furthermore, previous trials performed by the NUS research team showed insights into difficulties with adherence of gait sensors to the thigh. We have ideated and improved the mode of securing gait sensor using velcro based straps that house the sensor.

Figure 8 : Velcro Strap band housing Sensor

This provides a secure attachment of the sensor to the ankle and is an easier method of device attachment for MCI patients.

2.4 Gait Data Analysis

We are using previously collected Vicon based and Metawear (3-axis accelerometer) based data collected on healthy test participants to test the legacy code. Vicon is an industry-standard motion capture system that uses infrared cameras to detect reflective markers strategically placed on the participants. The Vicon software is then used to process the gait movement of participants with respect to time and space, identifying and quantifying gait features (Vicon Motion Systems, 2025).

During participant Vicon data collection, an older version of the Metabase sensor was attached to the participants' left and right ankle. The legacy code was developed to match the older sensor version with the Vicon data. The aim is to improve features and constants of the legacy code for the new Metabase sensor version (which replicates 3-axis accelerometer based research sensor), using the Vicon processed data for validation.

Through analysing the data sets provided, the following two improvements were made – feature detection within gait data sets, as well as reducing discrepancies within features.

Discrepancy #1: Feature Detection Within Gait Data Sets

Looking closely into the accelerometer data processing, each gait cycle will have a similar pattern and features (Figure 9). As mentioned previously, gait has certain distinct events. We need to ensure during analysis, our algorithm is able to distinguish these events from the processed data.

Figure 9 : Features in a Gait Cycle

Figure 10 shows a set of gait data collected from the right ankle of a healthy participant. This data set will be used in the report for further explanation of gait data analysis.

Figure 10: Gait Event Detection (Unmodified Code)

The legacy code is able to determine and mark most gait features. However, several features were missing. Specifically, terminal swing (TSW_onset) is incorrect in the 2nd gait, along with TSW_onset, Toe Off, and Heel Strike identifications missing in the fourth gait cycle.

Looking into the code flow in further detail, a potential root cause was identified.

Figure 11: Legacy Code Flow

The data processing to identify significant gait phases are dependent on the Terminal swing onset identified within each gait cycle. This identification is performed by a peak finding function.

Further analysis of the code showed that the terminal swing and terminal stance peaks in the 2nd gait cycle are significantly close to each other, as shown in Figure 12. Hence, the peak finding function ignores the 2nd peaks, causing misalignment of gait features in the following gait cycles.

Figure 12 : Right Shank Angle Peaks

To improve event detection, the minimum separation coefficient in the code was reduced from 0.75 to 0.2, resulting in the proper identification of gait features (Figure 13). The minimum separation coefficient distinguishes and decides the minimum duration between the same events over different cycles. By reducing this, the new code is able to accurately detect all missing features such as Right Heel Strike, Toe Off, and Terminal Swing Onset.

Figure 13 : Gait Event Detection (Modified Code)

Discrepancy #2: Differences Within Features Identified by Vicon and Code

Features such as cadence, step time, and stride length calculated by the legacy code closely match Vicon processed data with an average 5% error throughout all participants. However, features such as foot off and opposite foot off present high percentages of error (>100%), as seen in Figure 14. Hence, the primary aim is to identify strategies to reduce this error. The future aim would be to achieve accuracy close to Vicon-processed data.

Figure 14: Percentage (%) feature error comparison

Foot off indicates the percentage of the gait cycle during which a foot is lifted off the ground. Opposite foot off indicates the percentage for which the opposite foot is off the ground with respect to the current leg gait cycle (Vicon Motion Systems, 2019).

Comparing the processed data results output by the legacy code and Vicon, foot off (%) was consistently lower and opposite foot off (%) was consistently higher.

This ratio was constant, enabling the identification that foot off needs to be increased to approximately 280% of the value, and opposite foot off needs to be decreased to ~35% of the value. The ratio difference can be potentially attributed to changes due to different versions of the commercial Metabase sensor.

This modification of ratio in gait feature calculation reduced errors with each feature value (Foot off and Opp Foot off) across data sets averaging less than 50% error in comparison to Vicon data.

3. Speech

3.1 Literature Review

Speech is closely linked to cognitive processes such as memory, attention, and language, making it a sensitive indicator of early cognitive decline. In MCI, subtle changes can be observed, for example, people tend to speak more slowly, pause more often, and show reduced fluency. Their language also becomes simpler, with a more limited vocabulary and less coherent expression. Acoustic features such as pause duration, speech rate, jitter, and phonation stability can reliably distinguish MCI from healthy aging, reflecting the brain’s reduced efficiency in language planning and retrieval. As a result, speech analysis is a promising non-invasive tool for early MCI detection.  A summary of the literature findings are reflected in Table 4.

3.2 Data Collection Methods

Figure 15: Position of Sensor at Suprasternal Notch

(Jeong et al., 2021)

The sensor will be placed on the neck near the suprasternal notch (SN) as this position is near the vocal chords, hence enabling the sensor to collect distinct vibrations via the accelerometer. Since the sensor is placed directly on the skin and near the vocal muscles, there will be no leakage of signal from the vocal chords into the air.

Figure 16: Choice of Speech Protocol

Figure 16 provides a summary of speech tests used in industry. Tasks such as picture descriptions, open-ended questions, and everyday speech depend heavily on context, making the data less consistent and harder to analyse. As open-ended speech is difficult to standardise, it is difficult to compare speech patterns between patients.Backward counting was tested and it imposes a higher cognitive demand because it engages several executive functions simultaneously. It requires individuals to hold numbers in working memory and update them continuously. Furthermore, other tasks such as counting backwards poses challenges for sensor-based analysis as accelerometer signals require consistent timing while spontaneous speech varies in pitch and pacing. Unlike structured tasks which offer consistent and comparable data across participants, open tasks lack repeatability.

Since accelerometer data captures physical vibrations such as pauses and fillers, standardisation is important. From our review, we found that the caterpillar task gives the most consistent and reliable signals for analysis.

3.3 Speech Testing - Caterpillar Text

Since the focus is to determine MCI individuals based on vocal characteristics of speech rather than memory and comprehension demands, a constrained fixed passage task was designated as the testing protocol.

Figure 17 : The Caterpillar Passage (Annotated)

The caterpillar passage was specifically developed for a comprehensive assessment of motor-speech disorders. A relatively low verbal complexity and reading level (Flesh-Kincad Reading level of 5.0) ensures the passage can be read by most individuals. Other passage attributes encompass word repetition, enabling analysis of inconsistencies within repeated words); multiple intonations, enabling analysis of prosodic features such as fundamental frequency; along with a comprehensive inclusion of all English phonemes. Through vocal data collected from this moderate length passage, further features such as speech rate and number of pause can be measured (Patel et al., 2013).

These extractable vibrational vocal speech features make the Caterpillar Passage ideal for accelerometer-based mechanoacoustic data collection and analysis for individuals presenting with MCI.  

Vocal speech data via reading the caterpillar passage was collected on healthy individuals for further analysis at a sampling rate of 400Hz.

3.4 Speech Data Analysis

Figure 18: Vocal Data Processing Flow

Figure 18 summarises the overall method used to process vocal data.

Using the Metabase sensor, we collect 3 axis accelerometer data. This captures raw vocal vibrational data in the x, y and z axis at the suprasternal notch.  To analyse the various features, it is important to first convert the 3 axis vector to 1 axis by performing Euclidean normalisation (Matic et al., 2012), as reflected in Figure 19. This provides an orientation independent representation of the captured vocal vibrational magnitude.

Figure 19: Euclidean Normalisation

A bandpass filter is proposed to filter noise from the raw signal with cutoff frequencies between 100Hz to 200Hz, which are the vocal vibrational frequencies of both male and female, respectively. The built-in Python Butterworth filter function was used to perform the filtering of noise. Using the filtered signal, features such as pauses and speech rate are extractable (Subramanian et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024b).

The Nyquist-Shannon frequency sampling theorem suggests that a sampling frequency has to be twice that of the analog frequency being analysed, for an accurate reconstruction of analog to digital signals (Lai, 2003). Hence, a 400 Hz sampling frequency rate was proposed.

Figure 20 : Vocal data (caterpillar passage, healthy individual), accelerometer

This raw signal in Figure 20 undergoes Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT) to Figure 21. Conversion of the vocal signal to a frequency time domain enables quantitative analysis of significant vocal vibrational features such as fundamental frequency, jitter and shimmer in speech (Huang et al., 2024b) reflected in Table 5.

Figure 21 : Vocal data (caterpillar passage, healthy individual), FFT

By processing vocal accelerometer signals in the time domain (Figure 20) and frequency domain (Figure 21), significant parameters reflected in Figure 22 can be extracted, which may be indicative of MCI.

Figure 22: Quantitative parameters (Huang et al., 2024b)

Additional processing is commonly performed to convert vocal data to a Mel Frequency Plot, a visual representation of vocal data. This enables further differentiation between individuals with MCI and healthy individuals through identification of features such as Mel frequency coefficients (Huang et al., 2024b; Dubey et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2024)[1] 

To narrow down on suitable and informative features that are potentially indicative of MCI, the proposed idea is as follows. Collect vocal recordings of MCI patients performing constrained reading tasks from established sources and recreate/emulate the vocal recordings to collect the accelerometer data. Perform the same constrained reading task on healthy individuals and collect vocal accelerometer data for comparison. Once we have both datasets, we will compare to identify measurable acoustic differences to narrow down the parameters along with the thresholds.

4. Summary

Figure 25 : Overview of Speech and Gait Processes

The gait module uses the research sensor to record motion data, detect gait events, and extract features such as stride time and step variability. The data analysis method was validated against Vicon. The speech module uses the same sensor to capture vibration data, process it through filtering and spectrograms, and extract features such as pause duration and speech rate. Collectively, results from both modules will be used to screen for MCI, as seen in Figure 25.

Gait and speech are regulated by brain regions that are responsible for movement, coordination, and cognition. When these areas begin to deteriorate, subtle changes in walking and speaking often surface before memory problems become noticeable. Our project aims to prove that simple, low cost sensors, coupled with well-designed tasks and clear data analysis can detect early signs of MCI, making large-scale screening for MCI more accessible.
